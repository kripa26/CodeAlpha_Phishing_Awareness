Understanding the Threat

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of cyber attack where attackers disguise themselves as trusted entities to trick you into revealing sensitive information like passwords, credit card numbers, or personal data.

🎭

Deception

Attackers impersonate legitimate organizations — your bank, employer, or popular services like Netflix, Amazon, or PayPal — creating convincing fake messages.

🎯

Targeting

Phishing can be mass-sent to millions (spray and pray) or carefully targeted at specific individuals using personal information gathered from social media.

💰

Impact

Successful phishing attacks can lead to identity theft, financial loss, data breaches, ransomware infections, and compromised organizational systems.

Know Your Enemy

Types of Phishing Attacks

Phishing comes in many forms. Understanding each type helps you stay vigilant across all communication channels.

📧 Most Common

Email Phishing

The classic form — fake emails mimicking banks, tech companies, or colleagues. Often contains malicious links or attachments.

Example: "Your PayPal account has been limited. Click here to verify your identity."
🎯 Most Dangerous

Spear Phishing

Highly targeted attacks using personal information about the victim. Attackers research you on LinkedIn, social media, and company websites.

Example: "Hi [Your Name], here's the Q3 budget report you asked about in yesterday's meeting."
📱 Growing Threat

Smishing (SMS)

Phishing via text messages. Exploits the trust people have in SMS and the smaller screen that hides URL details.

Example: "USPS: Your package cannot be delivered. Update your address: bit.ly/xxxxx"
📞 Social Engineering

Vishing (Voice)

Phone-based phishing. Attackers call pretending to be tech support, the IRS, or your bank to extract information verbally.

Example: "This is Microsoft Support. We detected a virus on your computer. Let us remote in to fix it."
🐋 High Value

Whaling

Targets high-profile individuals like CEOs, CFOs, and executives. Uses business-specific language and urgent financial requests.

Example: "CEO to CFO: I need you to wire $50,000 to this vendor immediately. It's confidential."
👥 Hard to Detect

Clone Phishing

Duplicates a legitimate email you've already received, but replaces links or attachments with malicious ones. Very convincing.

Example: A copy of a real shipping notification, but the tracking link goes to a malware site.

Psychology of Attacks

Social Engineering Tactics

Phishing exploits human psychology, not technical vulnerabilities. Understanding these tactics makes you much harder to fool.

⏰

Urgency

"Your account will be deleted in 2 hours!" — Pressure makes people act without thinking. Real companies give reasonable deadlines.

🛡️ Defense: Take a breath. If it's truly urgent, call the company directly using a number you trust.
👔

Authority

"This is the CEO. I need you to transfer funds immediately." — We tend to obey authority figures without questioning them.

🛡️ Defense: Verify through a separate channel. Call or message the person directly to confirm.
😨

Fear

"We detected a virus on your computer!" — Fear overrides rational thinking and makes you follow instructions blindly.

🛡️ Defense: Legitimate security alerts don't ask you to call a phone number or install remote software.
🤝

Trust

Using familiar logos, names, and language to appear legitimate. "From: Amazon Customer Service" with perfect branding.

🛡️ Defense: Always check the actual email address and URLs, not just the display name and logos.
🎁

Greed / Curiosity

"You've won a free iPhone!" or "See who viewed your profile" — Appeals to our desire for rewards or secret knowledge.

🛡️ Defense: If something seems too good to be true, it probably is. Nobody gives away free iPhones.
💝

Helpfulness

"I'm from IT support, I need your password to fix the issue." — Exploits our natural desire to be helpful and cooperative.

🛡️ Defense: Legitimate IT staff will NEVER ask for your password. They can reset it without knowing it.
Stay Protected

Best Practices & Tips

Follow these actionable tips to protect yourself and your organization from phishing attacks.

01

Verify Before You Click

Hover over links to preview the URL before clicking. If unsure, navigate to the website directly by typing the address in your browser.

02

Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Even if your password is stolen, 2FA adds an extra layer of security. Use authenticator apps instead of SMS when possible.

03

Check the Sender's Email Address

Look at the actual email address, not just the display name. "Amazon Support" could be sending from "amaz0n@sketchy-domain.com".

04

Don't Share Sensitive Info via Email

No legitimate company will ever ask for your password, Social Security number, or full credit card details via email.

05

Keep Software Updated

Updates often contain security patches. Enable automatic updates for your OS, browser, and antivirus software.

06

Use a Password Manager

Password managers generate and store strong, unique passwords for every account. They also won't autofill on fake websites!

07

Report Suspicious Emails

Report phishing to your IT department, email provider, or forward to reportphishing@apwg.org. Your report helps protect others.

08

Be Skeptical of Urgency

Take a moment to think before acting on urgent messages. Real emergencies are handled through official channels, not random emails.

Learn from History

Real-World Case Studies

These high-profile phishing attacks show that anyone can be a target — from tech giants to government agencies.

2019

Google & Facebook — $100 Million Scam

A Lithuanian man impersonated a hardware manufacturer and sent fake invoices to Google and Facebook over two years. Both companies paid the fraudulent invoices, losing a combined $100 million before the scheme was discovered.

💡 Lesson: Always verify payment requests through a separate communication channel, especially for large amounts.
2020

Twitter Hack — Bitcoin Scam

Attackers used phone-based social engineering (vishing) to trick Twitter employees into giving up credentials to internal tools. They then hijacked accounts of Barack Obama, Elon Musk, and Bill Gates to promote a Bitcoin scam, stealing $120,000.

💡 Lesson: Even tech-savvy employees can fall for social engineering. Security awareness training is crucial for everyone.
2021

Colonial Pipeline — Ransomware Attack

A single compromised password (likely obtained through phishing) led to a ransomware attack that shut down the largest fuel pipeline in the US. The company paid $4.4 million in ransom, and fuel shortages affected the entire East Coast.

💡 Lesson: One compromised credential can bring down critical infrastructure. Use unique passwords and enable 2FA everywhere.
2023

MGM Resorts — Social Engineering

Attackers called MGM's IT help desk, impersonated an employee they found on LinkedIn, and convinced the help desk to reset the employee's credentials. This 10-minute phone call led to a $100 million loss and days of system outages.

💡 Lesson: Be cautious about what you share on social media. Attackers use public information to impersonate you.
